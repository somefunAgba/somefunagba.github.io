Learning-rate Design: A Trust-region Principle

A Modular View of Modern Optimizers

Page created: Sep 11 2025

Recall that momentum (Polyak, 1969) is a lowpass regularized stochastic gradient algorithm. One that applies a first-order lowpass filter to its stochastic gradient, before multiplying by a possibly optimal iteration-dependent learning-rate. Here, more specifically, we show how popular lowpass regularized stochastic gradient algorithms, Adaptive Moment Estimation (Adam) and Momentum Orthogonalized with Newton-Schultz (Muon) used to train modern AI models encode trust-region principles in their learning-rate object.

Form the setup in Part 1: Momentum as Lowpass Filter Design, recall that the learning-rates derived take the typical form $\gain{\alpha} \propto \lambda^{-1}$. More importantly, recall that the fastest mean-square convergence of the baseline confguration occurs when

\[\gain{\alpha} = \mu\,\tfrac{1}{\lambda}, \qquad \mu = \tfrac{1}{3}.\]

This motivates the use of learning-rate rules of the form $\gain{\alpha}=\gain{\mu}\,\lambda^{-1}$ with $0<\gain{\mu}<1$, which trades faster mean convergence for reduced error variance.

So What?: Modular Design and Trust Regions

Beyond this specific setup, the rule $\gain{\alpha} \propto \lambda^{-1}$ can be interpreted as expressing a normalization principle that scales parameter update steps inversely with $\lambda$, a characteristic measure (or scale) of the gradient magnitude. Under this interpretation, $\gain{\mu}$ is analogous to a trust-region radius (Conn et al., 2000), and controls the magnitude of the update step.

In general, this principle can be realized in practice via iterative data-driven substitutes $\lambda[t]$ for the fixed scale $\lambda$. Here, we assume that $\lambda^{-1}[t]$ is updated during each iteration in some appropriate manner. Interpreting $\lambda$ in this way cleanly separates momentum (gradient smoothing through a lowpass filter) and learning-rate selection (via gradient normalization) into modular components.

Therefore, stochastic gradient algorithms for more complicated stochastic setups can be designed to implement a modular strategy consisting of two principles:

  1. Gradient Filtering via a Lopass Filter: Designing \(\filter{\beta}, \filter{\gamma}\).
  2. Gradient Normalization via the Learning-rate: Scaling by a data-driven substitute for \(1/\lambda\).

Gradient Normalization

We have seen gradient filtering, the first module of this framework. The second module of the framework is the learning-rate subsystem.

We define the general update rule as

\[\rw[t+1] = \rw[t] - \gain{\alpha[t]}\,\rv[t]\]

where the learning-rate

\[\gain{\alpha[t]} = \gain{\mu}\,\lambda^{-1}[t]\]

effectively normalizes the filtered gradient to satisfy a trust-region constraint, using:

  • \(\gain{\mu}\) = fixed trust-region radius
  • \(\lambda[t]\) = possibly iterative gradient scale

Modular Design Principle

[1] Gradient Filter Design Choose \(\filter{\beta}, \filter{\gamma}\) to shape the gradient signal.

\[\rv[t] = \filter{\sH_{\beta,\gamma}}\{\rg[t]\},\]

This may be replaced by any appropriate gradient filter.

[2] Gradient Normalization: Apply a trust-region scaling $\gain{\mu}\,\lambda^{-1}[t]$ to normalize the gradient.

\[\Delta[t+1] = -\gain{\mu}\,\lambda^{-1}[t]\,\rv[t],\]

Normalize by \(\lambda^{-1}[t]\), to get \(\bar{\rv}[t] = \lambda^{-1}[t]\,\rv[t]\) then tak a step \(\Delta[t+1] = -\gain{\mu}\,\bar{\rv}[t]\) within a trust-region radius \(\gain{\mu}\).

This may be replaced by any appropriate trust-region principle.

[3] Parameter Update: Then update the parameter using the trust-region constrained $\Delta[t+1]$.

\[\rw[t+1] = \rw[t] + \Delta[t+1].\]

To briefly make this concrete, we will now show that this object-oriented modular approach generalizes to other settings beyond this simple setup. Specifically, popular modern deep learning optimizers like Adam and Muon implement these two principles.

Adam (Vectorized Formulation)

We now replace the scalar parameter update formulation in this setup with a per-coordinate parameter vector update form.

Let $\rw[t],\;\rg[t],\;\rv[t],\;\Delta[t+1]\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times 1}$, where $n \ge 1$, and let the learning rate be $\gain{\alpha[t]}\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times 1}$.

Scaling Choice

For each coordinate $i=1,\ldots,n$ of the parameter update step $\Delta[t+1,i] = \gain{\mu}\,\lambda^{-1}[t,i]\,\rv[t,i]$, choose the scale for normalization in the learning-rate vector as

\[\lambda[t,i] = C^{\frac{1}{2}}[t,i],\quad \text{where} \quad C[t] = \E\{\rg^2[t,i]\}.\]

which involves estimating the second moment or power of $\rg[t,i]$ for each coordinate.

Consequently, since \(\E\{\Delta^{2}[t+1,i]\} = \gain{\alpha}^2[t,i]\,\E\{\rv^{2}[t,i]\}\), and recall that \(\E\{\rv^{2}[t,i]\} \le \E\{\rg^{2}[t,i]\}\), then using $\gain{\alpha}^2[t,i]= \gain{\mu}^2\,\lambda^{-2}[t,i]=\gain{\mu}^2\,C^{-1}[t,i]$ yields

\[\E\{\Delta^{2}[t+1,i]\} \le \gain{\mu}^2,\]

with equality if $\gamma=\beta=0$.

Trust-region Constraint

This scaling choice can be interpreted as ensuring that under the parameter update step $\Delta[t+1] = \gain{\mu}\,\lambda^{-1}[t]\, \rv[t]$, each coordinate satisfies the trust-region constraint $\E \{ \Delta^{2}[t+1,i] \} \le \gain{\mu}^2$, and therefore it follows that overall at each iteration, the satisfied trust-region radius is

\[\E\{\|\Delta[t+1]\|_2^2\} \le n\,\gain{\mu}^2.\]

Realization

A simple but practical way to realize this scaling chocie is via an exponential moving average (EMA)

\[\begin{aligned} \rb[t,i] &= \rho\,\rb[t-1,i] + (1-\rho)\,\rg^{2}[t,i], \end{aligned}\]

where $\rho$ is a positive constant, close to but less than $1$. Additional refinements are possible. For example, dividing its output by $1-\rho^{t}$ removes the transient bias induced by initialization of the EMA recursion. Finally,

\[\begin{aligned} \lambda^{-1}[t,i] = \Bigl(\sqrt{\rb[t,i]} + \eps\Bigr)^{-1}, \end{aligned}\]

where $\eps$ is a small positive constant used to artificially ensure numerical stable computation of the reciprocal of $\sqrt{\rb[t,i]}$. The only moment statistic attempted to be estimated here is the per-coordinate gradient’s second moment.

Summary

The scaling choice in the learning-rate and its realization above performs coordinate-wise normalization, and corresponds to Adam (or RMSProp if $\gamma=\beta=0$) and we see that it directly enforces a per-coordinate trust-region constraint

\[\E\{\Delta^{2}[t+1,i]\} \le \gain{\mu}^2,\]

on the energy of the parameter step (update increments) controlled by a constant $\gain{\mu}$.

Muon (Matrix-valued Operator-level Formulation)

We now extend the parameter update form to a two-dimensional matrix operator-level update form. This operator-level formulation replaces scalar learning rates with linear operators acting on the gradient and replaces scalar second moments with covariance operators. Throughout, we use the Frobenius inner product

\[\langle A,B\rangle_F = \Tr(A^\intercal B), \quad \text{and}\quad \|A\|_F^2 = \langle A,A\rangle_F,\]

for any matrix $A,B\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times m}$, where $1 < n \le m < \infty$.

Let $\rw[t],\;\rg[t],\;\rv[t],\;\Delta[t+1]\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times m}$, and let the learning rate operator $\gain{\alpha[t]}\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ be symmetric and positive definite, such that $\gain{\alpha[t]}=\gain{\alpha[t]}^\intercal$.

Scaling Choice

For this formulation, we will choose the scaling rule for normalization in the parameter update step as

\[\begin{aligned} \lambda[t] = C^{\frac{1}{2}}[t],\quad \text{where} \quad C[t] &= {\rv[t]\,\rv[t]^\intercal}, \end{aligned}\]

The scaling rule is preserved as the matrix square root of a symmetric autocorrelation matrix of the filtered gradient. The inverse scale $\lambda^{-1}[t]=C^{-\frac{1}{2}}[t]$, the square root of the inverse of a symmetric positive-definite matrix $C[t]$. Therefore, this directly involves estimation of the inverse of the square-root of a matrix.

Trust-region Constraint

Consequently, $|\Delta[t+1]|_F^2 = \gain{\mu}^2 \,|C^{-\frac{1}{2}}[t]\,\rv[t]|_F^2 $, and since $|C^{-\frac{1}{2}}[t]\,\rv[t]|_F^2 = \operatorname{tr}(I_n)$, where $I_n\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ is an identity matrix, it follows that

\[\|\Delta[t+1]\|_F^2 = n\,\gain{\mu}^2,\]

which implies that the scaling choice can be interpreted as satisfying a constant trust-region constraint per iteration similar to the coordinate-wise scaling rule.

The coordinate-wise scaling rule enforces the trust-region constraint by measuring the expected Euclidean-norm as the energy of the parameter update step. In contrast, the matrix-valued scaling rule directly imposes a row-wise trust-region constraint measured in the Frobenius-norm. In both cases, the maximum possible trust-region radius is explicitly controlled by a scalar constant $\gain{\mu}$, acting per coordinate as previously seen, and per row for this matrix-valued formulation.

Implementation

A practical way to realize this matrix inverse square-root normalization is via the efficient polynomial method introduced by Lakić (Lakić, 1998). This method provides a family of stable, rapidly convergent recursions parameterized by a polynomial of order $j$, and includes several classical schemes as special cases. During implementation, invertibility of $C[t]$ can be artificially ensured by adding a scaled identity matrix $\epsilon\,I_n$ to $C[t]$.

Let $D[0] = I_n$, and $S[0] = u^{-1}\,C[t]\,D^2[0]$, where $u=|C[t]|_F$, and $u^{-1}\,C[t]$ is a normalized form of $C[t]$. The matrix inverse square-root recurrence from $k=1,\ldots,K$ is \(\begin{aligned} D[k+1] &= D[k]\,\varphi[k] \\ S[k+1] &= S[k]\,\varphi^2[k], \end{aligned}\)

where $\varphi[k]$ is a degree $j-1$ polynomial in $S[k]$ and $S[k] = u^{-1}C[t]\rmD^2[k]$. For the lowest polynomial order $j\ge2$ the corresponding $\varphi[k]$ is

\[\begin{aligned} j=2,\quad& \varphi[k] = \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(3\,I_n - S[k]\bigr).\\ j=3,\quad& \varphi[k] = \tfrac{1}{8}\bigl(15\,I_n - 10\,S[k] + 3S^2[k]\bigr)\\ j=4,\quad& \varphi[k] = \tfrac{1}{16}\bigl(35\,I_n - 35\,S[k] + 21\,S^2[k] - 5\,S^3[k]\bigr)\\ j=5,\quad& \varphi[k] = \tfrac{1}{128}\bigl(315\,I_n - 420\,S[k] + 378\,S^2[k] - 180\,S^3[k] + 35\,S^4[k] \bigr). \end{aligned}\]

This iterative scheme converges with Q-order $j$, and for low $j\in{3, 4, 5}$, a few iterations ($K \ge 3$) is typically sufficient. As ${k\to\infty}$, then the recurrence converges: $D[k]\to u^{\frac{1}{2}}C[t]^{-\frac{1}{2}}$, and $S[k]\to I_n$.

Notably, the classical Newton-Schulz iteration in (Higham, 2008) can be recovered as the special case $j=2$ of Lakić’s polynomial family.

Muon (Momentum Orthogonalized by Newton-Schulz) (Large et al., 2024; Bernstein & Newhouse, 2024) can be recovered by the single implementation of $\Delta[t+1] = -\gain{\mu}\,\lambda^{-1}[t]\, \rv[t] = -\gain{\mu}\,\bar{\rv}[t]$. Using a reduced singular value decomposition $\rv[t]=U[t]\Sigma[t]{V^\intercal}[t]$, the normalized filtered gradient $\bar{\rv}[t]=C^{-\frac{1}{2}}[t]\,\rv[t]=U[t]{V^\intercal}[t]$ can also be efficiently realized via a Newton-Schulz recurrence or similar higher-order polynomial recurrences.

Unified Insight

Both algorithms (Adam or Muon) follow a general update form:

\[\Delta[t+1] = \gain{\mu} \cdot \lambda^{-1}[t] \cdot \rv[t]\]

The only difference is how \(\lambda\) is defined.

New optimizers can be designed explicitly by (i) designing a gradient filter and (ii) choosing a learning-rate rule that encodes gradient normalization via a trust-region principle.

Conclusion: Your New Perspective on Learning and Optimizers

Instead of relying on physical metaphors, we can:

  • Treat gradients as signals
  • Design filters explicitly
  • Treat the learning-rate as a trust-region object
  • Design trust-region optimal principles

You have likely moved beyond seeing AI training as a black box of weights and biases. You now understand it as a sophisticated signal processing task. Momentum is the heartbeat of this process, a digital filter that separates the signal of the “truth” from the noise of the data.

3-Point Takeaway

  • Momentum is a Filter
    It is a first-order lowpass filter designed to attenuate high-frequency noise and preserve the underlying signal.

  • Poles and Zeros are the Design Knobs
    The difference between Heavy-Ball and Nesterov is determined by: \(\filter{\gamma}\)

  • Stability is the True Goal
    Momentum expands the stability region, allowing aggressive learning rates while maintaining safe updates within a trust region radius: \(\gain{\mu}\)

We will show in Part 3 that the optimal $\gain{\mu}$ that minimizes the mean-squared error dynamics of the stochastic error setup laid out in Part 1: Momentum as Lowpass Filter Design changes as a result of the kurtosis $\kappa$, tail-heaviness of the stochastic process.

Open Question

If popular algorithms encode this modular principle, then: What unexplored filtering plus trust-region design principles could unlock the next generation of optimizers?
