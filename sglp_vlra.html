A Variational Trust-Region Foundation for Learning Rate Annealing

Optimal Window Functions

In this framework, we reinterpret learning-rate annealing as extremal solutions to variational trust-region problems over a fixed optimization window. Specifically, we find that popular learning-rate schedules are not heuristics but optimal solutions to Dirichlet energy functionals over a vanishing trust-region.

Learning-rate schedules play a central role in stochastic gradient methods, especially in large-scale deep learning where training may span millions of iterations.

Despite their importance, commonly used schedules such as:

  • linear decay
  • cosine annealing

are largely seen as heuristics rather than derived from first principles.

At the same time, practical use of Adaptive Moment Estimation relies on:

  • gradient moment normalization
  • learning-rate annealing

but lacks a unified theoretical grounding.

In this work we develop a trust-region framework for deriving learning-rate annealing in the stochastic gradient algorithm. Each iteration is formulated as a trust-region subproblem that bounds the parameter’s step-change.

Under a fixed per-iteration trust-region radius, the resulting learning rate becomes inversely proportional to the square root of the second moment of the gradient, recovering the core structure of adaptive moment methods.

When the trust-region radius is allowed to vary and vanish over a finite training window, the problem becomes a variational optimization problem posed over the trust-region radius $\delta_t^2$ (or its square root). Minimizing different variational objectives yield distinct schedules:

  • Total variation of $\delta_t$ gives linear decay
  • Total variation of $\delta_t^2$ gives square-root linear decay
  • Regularized Total variation of $\delta_t$ gives raised-cosine decay

These functions can be approximated by a wide class of Lipschitz-smooth monotone functions. Finally, we show that incorporating annealing into momentum-based methods improves exponential stability of the update dynamics.

Trust-Region Formulation

Consider the stochastic gradient update along a coordinate $i$,

\[\rw[t+1,i] = \rw[t,i] - \gain{\alpha[t,i]}\,\rg[t,i]\]

where:

  • $\rw[t] \in \sR^n$ is the parameter vector,
  • $\rg[t] \in \sR^n$ is the stochastic gradient,
  • $\gain{\alpha[t]} \in \sR^n$ is the learning rate, and
  • $i \in \{1,\ldots,n\}$

Assumptions

Throughout, we use $\E[\cdot]$ to denote expectation with respect to the underlying stochastic process generating the gradients. No specific objective function, probabilistic structure, or distribution is assumed. We make only the following minimal assumptions for all $t \in \{0,\dots,\tau\}$ within a finite window of learning iterations where $\tau \gg 0$:

Lipschitz regularity The objective function $f(\rw[t,i])$ and its gradient are Lipschitz continuous.

Bounded second moment For each coordinate, the stochastic gradient admits a bounded second moment

\[\mathbb{E}[\rg^2[t,i]] \le \bar{\xi} < \infty, \quad \hbox{where}\quad\bar{\xi} := \sup_{0 \le t \le \tau} \E[\rg^2[t,i]]\]

is a finite and strictly positive constant.

Trust-Region Radius

Define the step-change \(\Delta[t+1,i] = \rw[t+1,i] - \rw[t,i].\)

The second moment of the parameter step-change,

\[\mathbb{E}[\Delta^2[t+1,i]] = \gain{\alpha^2[t,i]} \, \mathbb{E}[\rg^2[t,i]]\]

is well-defined for all $t$. This variable is used as a trust-region measure in this analysis.

Specifically, we interpret this quantity as the iterative trust-region radius

\[\delta_t^2 \equiv \mathbb{E}[\Delta^2[t+1,i]]\]

of the learning algorithm. This can also be written as

\[\gain{\alpha^2[t,i]} \, \mathbb{E}[\rg^2[t,i]] = \delta_t^2.\]

Learning-Rate

It can be observed that the square-root of the varying trust-region radius $\gain{\delta_t}$ directly determines the learning-rate, that is

\[\gain{\alpha[t,i]} = \frac{\gain{\delta_t}}{\sqrt{ \mathbb{E}[\rg^2[t,i]}}.\]

This recovers the structure of adaptive moment estimation.

Vanishing Trust-Region Radius Problem

At each learning iteration, it is desired that the learning algorithm’s step-changes is bounded within a maximum trust region radius $\rho \in \bigl(0,1\bigr)$, such that

\[\delta_t^2 =\mathbb{E}[\Delta^2[t+1,i]] \le \rho^2,\]

and that

\[\gain{\delta_t} \to 0 \quad \text{as } t \to \tau.\]

Motivation

A vanishing trust-region radius would artificially guarantee asymptotic stability of $\Delta[t+1,i]\to 0$ in the mean and mean-square, a classical stability requirement in stochastic approximation (Ge et al., 2018).

This suggests

  • suppression of oscillations in the step-change due to gradient noise,
  • conservative step-changes near a local optima as $t\to\tau$.

Alternative Case: Fixed Trust-Region Radius

If $\gain{\delta_t} = \gain{\mu}$, a positive constant for all iterations, then:

\[\gain{\alpha[t,i]} = \frac{\gain{\mu}}{\sqrt{\mathbb{E}[\rg^2[t,i]]}}\]
Equivalent Trust-Region Subproblem

The learning rate arises as the solution to:

\[\min_{\gain{\alpha[t,i]}} \; \mathbb{E}[\Delta[t+1,i] \rg[t,i]] \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \mathbb{E}[\Delta^2[t+1,i]] = \mu^2\]

using a first-order approximation:

\[\mathbb{E}[f(\rw[t+1,i]) - f(\rw[t,i])] \approx \mathbb{E}[\rg[t,i] \, \Delta[t+1,i]].\]

This Case: Vanishing Trust-Region Radius

In this framework, $\delta_t$ vanishes over the finite training window \(t \in \{0, \dots, \tau\}.\)

Consequently, the initial value and final value of $\delta_t$ are fixed constants, so that the boundary conditions of $\delta_t$ correspond to

\[\delta_0 = \mu, \quad \text{and} \quad \delta_{\tau} = 0.\]
Variational Formulation

To obtain unique variational functions that provide the smoothest possible decay of the trust-region radius, we pose total variational problems over $\delta_t$ (or $\delta_t^2$) within the optimization window.

Minimizing total variation ensures the iterative trust-region radius of the learning algorithm does not to vanish abruptly or faster than necessary during learning as $\delta_t \to 0$.

We now solve for optimal $\delta_t$ functions under first-order smoothness constraints via a Dirichlet Energy functional, which measures the “roughness” or total variation of a signal.

Linear Decay

Goal: Minimize the Total variation of $\delta_t$

Variational Functional

\[\min_{0\le\delta_t\le\mu} \sum_{t=1}^{\tau} (\delta(t) - \delta(t-1))^2\]

Unique Solution

Linear decay,

\[\delta_t = \mu \, \bigl(1 - \tfrac{t}{\tau} \bigr)\]

is the unique minimizer of the total variation of $\delta_t$.

Square-root Linear Decay

Goal: Minimize the Total variation of $\delta^2_t$

Variational Functional

\[\min_{0\le\delta^2_t\le\mu^2} \sum_{t=1}^{\tau} (\delta^2(t) - \delta^2(t-1))^2\]

Unique Solution

Square-root Linear decay,

\[\delta_t = \mu \, \sqrt{1 - \tfrac{t}{\tau}}\]

is the unique minimizer of the total variation of $\delta^2_t$. This is equivalent to

\[\delta^2_t = \mu \, \bigl(1 - \tfrac{t}{\tau} \bigr).\]

Cosine Annealing

Goal: Minimize a Tikhonov Regularized Total variation of $\delta_t$

Variational Functional

The general solution of $\delta_t$ that produced linear decay can be written in affine form as

\[\delta_t = a + b\,u_t\]

where $u_t$ is an iterative function of $t$, and $a,b\in\sR$, and the fixed boundary conditions $\delta_0= \mu$, $\delta_\tau=0$ correspond to $u_0=\tfrac{\mu-a}{b}$, and $u_\tau=-\tfrac{a}{b}$. This shows that the non-trivial underlying functional form of $\delta_t$ is captured by a shaping function $u_t$ while $a,b$ are trivial shift and scaling constants determined by the fixed boundary conditions. The minimization problem of $\delta_t$, then becomes equivalent to \begin{align}\label{udiscfcn} \begin{aligned} \min_{u_t\in [u_0,\,u_\tau]} \quad\sum_{t=1}^{\tau} \bigl(u_t - u_{t-1}\bigr)^2. \end{aligned} \end{align}

Here, we now define a regularized minimization problem directly on $u_t$. Due to fixed boundary conditions, the variational objective term $\bigl(u_t - u_{t-1}\bigr)^2$ is essentially defined over transition pairs $(t-1,\,t)$ within the interior of the window $t\in{1,\dots,\tau-1}$. Therefore, any added quadratic energy regularization should be expressed in a compatible pairwise form in order to maintain consistency with the transition-based structure of the objective term (Grady & Polimeni, 2010).

A natural choice is the symmetric quadratic form \(\tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(u^2_t +u^2_{t-1}\bigr)\), which is analogous to Tikhonov regularization and introduces a shape-smoothing penalty (Yue et al., 2016). We therefore define the regularized energy minimization problem as

\[\begin{aligned} \min_{u_t\in\:[u_0\mkern-5mu,\,u_\tau\mkern-5mu]} \quad \sum_{t=1}^{\tau} \bigl(u_t - u_{t-1}\bigr)^2 + \lambda\, \Biggl[\sum_{t=1}^{\tau}\tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(u^2_{t}+u^2_{t-1}\bigr) - \tilde{c}\Biggr], \end{aligned}\]

where $\lambda \in \sR$ is the penalty constant or Lagrange multiplier associated with the regularization term, and $\tilde{c} > 0$ is a fixed constant representing the allowable total quadratic energy.

By substituting $u_t = \frac{\delta_t-a}{b}$ the equivalent regularized minimization problem in terms of $\delta_t$ can be obtained.

Unique Solution

\[\delta_t = \mu \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[1 + \cos\bigl(\pi \tfrac{t}{\tau}\bigr)\bigr] = \mu \cos^2\bigl(\tfrac{\pi\,t}{2\,\tau}\bigr)\]

equivalent to a raised cosine window

Unifying Structure

All solutions share the structural form

\[\delta_t = \mu \,\psi_t\]

with

  • $\psi_0 = 1$
  • $\psi_{\tau} = 0$
  • $\psi_t$ monotonically decays

Approximating Families of Smooth Window Functions

Smooth Prototypes

Consider a continuously differentiable (smooth) prototype function $\phi:[0,1] \to[0,1]$ defined over a fixed window of iterations $t\in{0,\dots,\tau}$ by \begin{align} \psi_t=\phi\left(\tfrac{t}{\tau}\right), \end{align} with the endpoint constraints $\phi(0)=1, \phi(1)=0$.

For the interior $t\in{1,\dots,\tau-1}$, denote $x_t = \tfrac{t}{\tau}$ as an ordered uniform discretization of $(0,1)$ over which $\phi(x)$ decays monotonically. Assume further that $\phi’(x)$, the first-derivative of $\phi$ with respect to $x$ exists for all $x\in(0,1)$ and that the average squared variation induced by $\phi$ over the discretization remains bounded as the fixed window length $\tau$ grows.

For any $t\in{1,\dots,\tau-1}$, by the mean-value theorem, there exists a $\bar{x} \in\bigl[\tfrac{t-1}{\tau},\tfrac{t}{\tau}\bigr] $, such that $\psi_t-\psi_{t-1} = \phi\left(\tfrac{t}{\tau}\right) - \phi\left(\tfrac{t-1}{\tau}\right) = \phi’(\bar{x}) \left( \tfrac{t}{\tau} - \tfrac{t-1}{\tau} \right) = \phi’(\bar{x}) \frac{1}{\tau} $.

Plugging into the Dirichlet energy functional over $\delta_t$, we obtain \begin{align}\label{gendiri} \gD_{\tau, \psi_t} = \mu^2\sum_{t=1}^{\tau} \bigl(\psi_t-\psi_{t-1}\bigr)^2 = \biggl(\frac{\mu}{\tau}\biggr)^2\sum_{t=1}^{\tau} \bigl(\phi’(\bar{x})\bigr)^2, \end{align} Define the normalized Dirichlet energy as the average function, \begin{align}\label{smconst} L = \frac{1}{\tau}\sum_{t=1}^{\tau} \bigl(\phi’(\bar{x})\bigr)^2. \end{align} Re-expressing in terms of (\ref{smconst}), then (\ref{gendiri}) becomes \begin{align}\label{gendirired} \gD_{\tau, \psi_t} = {\mu^2}\,L\,\tau^{-1}, \end{align} where the definition of $L$ in (\ref{smconst}) captures the average squared variation of $\phi$ over the discretized interval. This characterizes the effective smoothness of the prototype function within the Dirichlet energy functional. Furthermore, it shows that the smoothness behavior of a Dirichlet-energy minimizing $\phi(x)$ is determined up to the scaling factor $L$, which encodes variation of $\phi(x)$ over the interval.

\[L^2 = \int_{0}^{1} \bigl(\phi'(x)\bigr)^2.\]

For $x=\tfrac{t}{\tau}$,

  • $\phi(x)=1-x$, $L=1$, recovers linear decay
  • $\phi(x)=\cos^2(\pi x/2)$, $L=\tfrac{\pi^2}{8} $ recovers cosine annealing

The Dirichlet energy of the square-root linear decay is defined over $\delta_t^2$, so approximating it is equivalent to approximating linear decay and taking the square-root.

Consequence

Smooth prototype functions like polynomial tapers, sigmoid functions, exponential decay can be used to approximate the smothness quantity $L$ of either the linear decay or cosine annealing function.

However only the derived ones correspond to an optimality notion of minimum variational energy.

Prototype-Preserving Reparameterizations

To transform these half-window prototypes into various shapes that have been reported in the literature, we can apply prototype-preserving reparameterizations of $x$ via interval shifts.

For any $u\in[0,1]$, the interval transform $q_i(u) = 2u - 1$ represents a mapping to $[-1,1]$.

Fold

Using this $q_i$ as a base, we can obtain different hat-shaped tapers using the interval transform \begin{align}\label{fwinshift} x=q_0(\tilde{x},\tilde{m}) = \left\lvert \frac{q_i(\tilde{x}) - q_i(\tilde{m})}{1 - \mathrm{sign}(q_i(\tilde{x}))\,q_i(\tilde{m})} \right\vert, \end{align} which takes a $\tilde{x}\in[0,1]$ and a desired peak point $0\le\tilde{m}<1$, both within the same half-window interval. The transformation stretches the half-space of $q_i(\tilde{x})$ around $q_i(\tilde{m})$ and folds the result back onto $[0,1]$. When $\tilde{m}=0$, this interval transform (\ref{fwinshift}) reduces to the identity mapping $x=\tilde{x}$, producing a half-taper defined by $\phi(x)$. In contrast, when $\tilde{m}=0.5$ the same transformation yields a full symmetric taper using the same prototype $\phi(x)$.

Hold

To induce the effect of a constant region of width $0 \le \tilde{\varepsilon} < 1$, we can map $\tilde{x}\in[0,1]$ to $[\tilde{\varepsilon},1]$ via \begin{align}\label{eshift} x = q_1(\tilde{x}, \tilde{\varepsilon}) = \max\left(0,\frac{\tilde{x}-\tilde{\varepsilon}}{1-\tilde{\varepsilon}}\right), \end{align} so that for all $\tilde{x}\le\tilde{\varepsilon}$, the window remains at $\phi({x})=\phi(0)=1$.

Finally, composing the two maps for any $0 \le \tilde{m},\tilde{\varepsilon} < 1$ yields an hybrid interval transform \begin{align}\label{xcmap} x = q_1\bigl(q_0(\tilde{x}, \tilde{m}), \tilde{\varepsilon}\bigr) \end{align} leading to a family of regular window shapes generated by a single underlying $\phi(x)$.

Improved Exponential Stability for Momentum

When generalized to the momentum-based form, the update rule is driven by a first-order filtered gradient.

We now show that in this case, vanishing $\delta_t$ improves the exponential stability of $\Delta[t+1,i]\to 0$

Popular momentum-based methods can be rewritten using a first-order filter with a single pole $0\le\beta <1$ and zero $\gamma<\beta$:

\[H_{\beta,\gamma}(z) = \eta \frac{1 - \gamma z^{-1}}{1 - \beta z^{-1}}\]

The general update rule with \(\rv[t,i]=\sH_{\beta,\gamma}\{\rg[t,i]\}\) then becomes

\[\rw[t+1,i] = \rw[t,i] - \gain{\alpha[t,i]}\,\rv[t,i]\]

Equivalently,

\begin{align}\label{eq:oneline} \rw[t+1,i] = \rw[t,i] \,+\,r[t,i]\,\beta\,\bigl(\rw[t,i]-\rw[t-1,i]\bigr) \,-\,\gain{\alpha[t,i]}\,\eta\,\bigl(\rg[t,i] - \gamma\,\rg[t-1,i]\bigr) \end{align} where the ratio $r[t,i] = \gain{\alpha[t,i]}/\gain{\alpha[t-1,i]}$, the step-change dynamics becomes

\[\Delta[t+1,i] = \beta\,r[t,i] \Delta[t,i] + \gain{\alpha[t,i]}\,\eta \bigl(\gamma\,\rg[t-1] - \rg[t,i])\]

Vanishing Window Ratios and Exponential Stability

Recall that \(\gain{\delta_t} = \mu \,\psi_t,\)

\[\gain{\alpha[t,i]} =\frac{\gain{\delta_t}}{\sqrt{ \mathbb{E}[\rg^2[t,i]}}.\]

Also,

\[r[t,i] = \frac{\gain{\alpha[t,i]}}{\gain{\alpha[t-1,i]}},\]

therefore $ r[t,i] \propto \frac{\delta_t}{\delta_{t-1}}. $

First, we show that if the ratio ${\delta_t}/{\delta_{t-1}} \to 0$ then we must have $\delta_t\to0$. Next, we show that ${\delta_t}\to 0$ strictly faster than ${\delta_t}/{\delta_{t-1}} \to 0$. Finally, we show that these vanishing window ratios induce a stronger exponential contraction of the update step.

In particular, we show that ${\delta_t}/{\delta_{t-1}} \to 0$ corresponds to improving the exponential stability property of the update step $\Delta[t+1, i]$ using momentum. This is because the contribution of the stochastic gradient term $\bigl(\rg[t,i] - \gamma\,\rg[t-1,i]\bigr)$ is increasingly dampened by a ${\delta_t}\to 0$ vanishing strictly faster than ${\delta_t}/{\delta_{t-1}} \to 0$ can vanish.

\[\delta_t \le \frac{\delta_t}{\delta_{t-1}} \le \eps,\quad \epsilon\in(0,1),\quad t \ge t'\]

Iterating from $t=t’$, we obtain for all $t \ge t’$, approximately yields \begin{align}\label{eq:onelndynauto1} \absv{\Delta[t+1, i]} \le \epsilon^{t+1-t’}\,\beta^{t+1-t’}\,\frac{\rd[t’-1,i]}{\rd[t,i]}\,\absv{\Delta[t’, i]}, \end{align}

artificially inducing improved exponential stability of the learning dynamics.

Interpretation

Introducing annealing via $\psi_t$:

  1. asymptotically shrinks the step energy,
  2. then shrinks the ratio $r_t$,
  3. which implies strictly stronger exponential stability of the momentum-based update step.

Takeaways

Learning-rate schedules are not heuristics. They emerge as optimal solutions to smooth contraction of a vanishing trust region.

It is about controlling the variation of the trust-region radius measured by the step-changes of the learning algorithm.

Unification

  • learning-rate schedules = trust-region window functions
  • adaptive moment estimation = fixed trust-region radius solution
  • annealing = vanishing trust-region radius solution
