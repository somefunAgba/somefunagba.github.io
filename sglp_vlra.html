A Variational Trust-Region Foundation for Learning-Rate Annealing

Optimal Window Functions

Sep 11 2025

Please cite this page if you use information from these notes for your work, research, or anything that requires academic or formal citation. Oluwasegun Somefun. “A Variational Trust-Region Foundation for Learning-Rate Annealing.” 2025.

In this framework, we reinterpret learning-rate annealing as extremal solutions to variational trust-region problems over a fixed optimiation window. Specifically, we find that popular learning-rate schedules are not heuristics but optimal solutions to Dirichlet energy functionals over a vanishing trust-region.

We find that Linear decay, cosine annealing, and square-root decay are not heuristic choices.
They are optimal solutions to variational trust-region problems over a fixed optimization window.

Key Idea

  • Fix the trust-region → adaptive moment estimation
  • Let it vanish → annealing schedules

On Learning-rate schedules

Learning-rate schedules play a central role in stochastic gradient methods, especially in large-scale deep learning where training may span millions of iterations.

Despite their importance, commonly used schedules such as:

  • linear decay
  • cosine annealing

are largely heuristic rather than derived from first principles.

At the same time, adaptive methods like Adam rely on:

  • gradient moment normalization
  • learning-rate annealing

but lack a unified theoretical grounding.

We reinterpret stochastic gradient descent through a trust-region lens:

This work develops a trust-region framework for deriving optimal learning-rate schedules in stochastic gradient methods. Each iteration is formulated as a trust-region subproblem that bounds the parameter increment.

Under a fixed per-iteration trust-region radius, the resulting learning rate becomes inversely proportional to the square root of the second moment of the gradient, recovering the core structure of adaptive moment methods.

When the trust-region radius is allowed to vary and vanish over a finite training window, the problem becomes a variational optimization problem posed over the trust-region radius or its square root. Different smoothness objectives yield distinct schedules:

  • Minimizing total variation of $\delta_t$ → linear decay
  • Regularized variation → raised cosine schedule
  • Minimizing variation of $\delta_t^2$ → square-root linear decay

These schedules can be approximated by a wide class of Lipschitz-smooth monotone functions. Finally, incorporating these schedules into momentum methods improves exponential stability of the update dynamics.

1. Trust-Region Formulation

Consider the coordinate-wise update

\(w_{t+1,i} = w_{t,i} - \alpha_{t,i}\,g_{t,i}\) where:

  • $w_{t,i}$ is the parameter
  • $g_{t,i}$ is the stochastic gradient
  • $\alpha_{t,i}$ is the learning rate

Assumptions

(1) Lipschitz regularity

The objective $f(w_{t,i})$ and its gradient are Lipschitz continuous.

(2) Finite second moment

\[\mathbb{E}[g_{t,i}^2] \le \bar{\xi} < \infty\]

3. Trust-Region Radius

Define the step increment:

\[\Delta_{t+1,i} = w_{t+1,i} - w_{t,i} = -\alpha_{t,i} g_{t,i}\]

Its second moment:

\[\mathbb{E}[\Delta_{t+1,i}^2] = \alpha_{t,i}^2 \, \mathbb{E}[g_{t,i}^2]\]

We define the trust-region radius:

\[\delta_t^2 \equiv \mathbb{E}[\Delta_{t+1,i}^2]\]

so that:

\[\alpha_{t,i}^2 \, \mathbb{E}[g_{t,i}^2] = \delta_t^2\]

Trust-Region Constraint

We enforce:

\[\delta_t^2 \le \rho^2, \quad 0 < \rho < 1\]

and impose a vanishing condition:

\[\delta_t \to 0 \quad \text{as } t \to \tau\]

Special Case: Fixed Radius

If $\delta_t = \mu$ (constant), then:

\[\alpha_{t,i} = \frac{\mu}{\sqrt{\mathbb{E}[g_{t,i}^2]}}\]

This recovers the structure of adaptive moment methods.

Trust-Region Subproblem

The learning rate arises as the solution to:

\[\min_{\alpha_{t,i}} \; \mathbb{E}[\Delta_{t+1,i} g_{t,i}] \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \mathbb{E}[\Delta_{t+1,i}^2] = \mu^2\]

Using a first-order approximation:

\[\mathbb{E}[f(w_{t+1,i}) - f(w_{t,i})] \approx \mathbb{E}[g_{t,i} \, \Delta_{t+1,i}]\]

Structural Interpretation

  • $\delta_t$ controls the allowed step energy
  • $\sqrt{\mathbb{E}[g^2]}$ normalizes stochastic gradient scale

This is exactly the structure of adaptive moment estimation

5. Vanishing Trust-Region Framework

We consider a finite training window:

\[t \in \{0, \dots, \tau - 1\}\]

with boundary conditions:

\[\delta_0 = \mu, \quad \delta_{\tau-1} = 0\]

Motivation

A vanishing trust region ensures:

  • $\Delta_{t+1,i} \to 0$
  • stability in mean and mean-square
  • suppression of oscillations from gradient noise
  • conservative updates near optima

6. Variational Formulation

To obtain a smooth decay, we pose variational problems over:

  • $\delta_t$
  • $\delta_t^2$

We minimize total variation to ensure:

  • no abrupt shrinkage
  • no unnecessarily rapid decay

3. Three Optimal Window Functions

We now solve for $\delta_t$ under the smoothness constraints.

I. Linear Decay

Goal: Minimize variation of $\delta_t$

  • Functional \(\sum_{t=1}^{\tau-} (\delta(t) - \delta(t-1))^2\)

  • Resulting Form \(\delta_t \propto 1 - \frac{t}{\tau}\)

Linear Decay is the unique minimizer of the Dirichlet Energy functional, which measures the “roughness” or total variation of a signal.

Interpretation

  • minimal total variation
  • uniform contraction
  • no bias toward early or late decay

II. Cosine Annealing

Regularized variation, balance variation and magnitude

We solve

  • Functional

    \[\sum_{t=1}^{\tau-1} (\delta(t) - \delta(t-1))^2+ \lambda\]

  • Resulting Form

\[\delta_t \propto \frac{1}{2}\left(1 + \cos\left(\frac{\pi t}{\tau}\right)\right)\]

Interpretation

  • slow initial decay
  • rapid contraction near the boundary
  • equivalent to a raised cosine window
  • explicitly minimizes a regularized energy functional

III. Square-root Linear Decay

Minimize variation of $\delta_t^2$

When the Dirichlet energy minimization is applied to the squared trust-region radius \(\delta^2(t)\) rather than \(\delta(t)\), we arrive at the square-root linear form.

  • Functional \(\sum_{t=1}^{\tau} (\delta^2(t) - \delta^2(t-1))^2\)

  • Resulting Form \(\delta_t \propto \sqrt{1 - \frac{t}{\tau}}\)

Interpretation

  • preserves magnitude of the step energy for longer
  • sharper contraction near the end
  • optimal under energy on squared radius

4. Unifying Structure

All solutions share the form

\[\delta_t = \mu \,\omega_t\]

with

  • $\omega_0 = 1$
  • $\omega_{\tau-1} = 0$
  • $\omega_t$ monotonically decays

8. General Function Class

These schedules can be approximated by functions that are:

  • monotone decreasing
  • Lipschitz continuous
  • endpoint-constrained

Accuracy depends on a smoothness constant.

9. Stability Interpretation

When combined with momentum methods:

  • updates form a first-order filter
  • vanishing $\delta_t$ improves exponential stability
  • the dynamics become increasingly damped

10. Contributions

  1. Trust-region formulation of learning rates
  2. Variational derivation of annealing schedules
  3. Characterization of admissible smooth function classes
  4. Stability interpretation of annealing

Key Insight

Learning-rate schedules are not heuristics—they emerge as optimal solutions to smooth contraction of a vanishing trust region.

It is about controlling the variation of the trust-region.

Thematic Takeaways

  1. Variational Scaling

    \[O((\tau)^{-1})\]

  2. Forced Convergence

    By satisfying \(\delta(\tau - 1) = 0,\) these schedules artificially enforce convergence.

  3. Spectral Regularization

    Three popular schedules are an extremal solution to a smoothness problem, suggesting that the choice of schedule is actually a choice of how we regularize the update trajectory of the learning algorithm.

Dirichlet energy bound

Using mean-value theorem,

\[|\omega_t - \omega_{t-1}| \le \frac{L}{\tau-1}\]

so

\[\mathcal{D} = \sum (\omega_t - \omega_{t-1})^2 \le \mu^2 L^2 (\tau-1)^{-1}\]

Consequence

Any smooth prototype function generates a valid annealing schedule
but only the derived ones are optimal (minimum energy)

Examples

  • $\phi(x)=1-x$ → linear
  • $\phi(x)=\cos^2(\pi x/2)$ → cosine
  • polynomial tapers, sigmoid functions, exponential decay → approximations

Extending the Framework: Lipschitz-Smooth Prototypes and Interval Shifts

The three optimal schedules belong to a broader class of Smooth Prototype Functions \(\phi(x)\). Any monotonically decaying function on the interval \([0, 1]\) where \(\phi(0)=1,\quad \phi(1)=0\) can serve as a window, provided its first derivative is Lipschitz-continuous.

To transform these half-window prototypes into complex shapes used in modern architectures, we apply Interval Shifts:

  • Symmetric Mapping (\(q_0\))
    \(x = |2\tilde{x} - 1|\)

  • Constant Regions (\(q_1\))
    \(x = \max\left(0, \frac{\tilde{x} - \tilde{\epsilon}}{1 - \tilde{\epsilon}}\right)\)

By composing these transformations \(q_1(q_0(\tilde{x}))\), we can rigorously derive the Trapezoidal and Tukey windows.

6. The Impact on Learning Dynamics: Exponential Stability

Momentum is a filter:

\[H_{\beta,\gamma}(z) = \eta \frac{1 - \gamma z^{-1}}{1 - \beta z^{-1}}\]

Update rule

\[w_{t+1,i} = w_{t,i} - \alpha_{t,i}\,v_{t,i}\]

Step dynamics

\[\Delta_{t+1,i} = \beta \frac{\delta_t}{\delta_{t-1}} \Delta_{t,i} + \delta_t \cdot (\text{gradient input})\]

7. Vanishing Window Ratios and Stability

Define ratio

\[r_t = \frac{\delta_t}{\delta_{t-1}}\]

Key fact

If

\[r_t \to 0\]

then vanishing ratio forces the update step

\[\delta_t \to 0 \quad \text{faster}\]

Consequence: Asymptotic dynamics

Dynamics reduce to

\[\Delta_{t+1,i} \approx \beta r_t \Delta_{t,i}\]

Contraction

\[|\Delta_t| \le (\beta \epsilon)^t\]

for some $\epsilon < 1$

artificially inducing Exponential Stability.

Interpretation

Annealing introduces:

  1. shrinks step energy ($\delta_t$)
  2. shrinking ratio decay ($r_t$)

→ strictly stronger exponential stability of the step

8. Takeaways

Unification

  • learning-rate schedules = trust-region window functions
  • adaptive methods = fixed-radius solution
  • annealing = vanishing-radius solution

Structure

  • linear / cosine / sqrt-linear = variational optima
  • momentum = lowpass filter
  • annealing = time-varying gain control

9. Final Perspective

Wrong framing

“Which schedule works best?”

Correct framing

“What notion of smoothness should govern the contraction of the trust-region?”
