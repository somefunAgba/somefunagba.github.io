Momentum as Lowpass Filter Design

Fastest mean convergence can be achieved without Momentum.

Sep 11 2025

Here we introduce the concept of momentum (Polyak, 1969) as a lowpass regularized stochastic gradient algorithm. One that applies a first-order lowpass filter to its stochastic gradient, before multiplying by a possibly optimal iteration-dependent learning rate. For analytic purpose, we will study a minimal stochastic system, for which the algorithm’s hyperparameters are assumed time-invariant. More specifically, we show how Nesterov’s momentum arise as the solution to the fastest mean error convergence of this problem. More importantly, we show that structurally, the Plain stochastic gradient method, Heavy-Ball momentum, and Nesterov’s momentum correspond fundamentally to different filter designs of the same algorithm.

Understanding Momentum as Lowpass Regularization

Traditionally, the way we talk about training neural networks is often rooted in classical mechanics. We describe momentum as a ball rolling down a hilly landscape, gaining inertia to carry it over local bumps. Alternatively, another popular interpretation is that momentum estimates the first moment of a gradient. While these physical analogies are often intuitive, they obscure the underlying discrete, iteration-level mechanics of the learning algorithm.

Our central claim is that a literal mathematical interpretation already exists via classical signal processing tools related to first-order lowpass filters. Momentum is mathematically equivalent to passing stochastic gradients through a first-order linear lowpass filter.

The filter, denoted \(\filter{\sH_{\beta,\gamma}}\) is configured via two variables, its pole \(\filter{\beta}\) and zero \(\filter{\gamma}\). By shifting the pole and zero locations of the filter, we can create different flavors of momentum.

We will soon see that this filter design interpretation provides a principled dynamical regularization perspective into the behavior of classic momentum variants, and can improve the way we design stochastic gradient learning algorithms for training AI models.

Setup: A Minimalist Stochastic Error Model

To understand what fundamentally drives learning dynamics, it helps to strip the system down to its bare essentials. To make our position concrete and analytically transparent, we deliberately adopt a minimalist setting. By stripping away the tensor algebra of high-dimensional neural networks, we expose the error dynamics and underlying structure of stochastic gradient learning in its simplest form.

Consider a single parameter model defined by the twice-differentiable weighted quadratic function

\[f(\rw[t]) = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\psi^2[t]\,\varepsilon^2[t],\]

where

  • \(\rw[t] \in \sR^{1}\) (Parameter) denotes the model parameter at a current iteration \(t\).
  • \(\varepsilon[t]\) (Error) defined as \(\varepsilon[t]=\rw[t] - \rw^\star\) represents distance of the current parameter value from its optimum value, \(\rw^\star\). The current error change due to a parameter update is defined as \(\Delta[t+1]=\varepsilon[t+1]- \varepsilon[t]\).
  • \(\psi[t] \in \sR^{1}\) (Data Process) is a realization of a zero-mean, wide-sense stationary stochastic data process for which its kurtosis, \(\kappa\ge1\) exists. We also assume that \(\psi[t]\) is statistically independent of both \(\varepsilon[t]\) and \(\psi[l]\), $l \neq t$.
  • \(\lambda\) (Scale) denotes the second moment of the stochastic process, defined by \(\mathbb{E}[\psi^2[t]]=\lambda\), and \(\mathbb{E}[\psi^4[t]]=\kappa\,\lambda^2\), \(\lambda>0\).

Differentiating \(f(\rw[t])\) with respect to \(\rw[t]\) reveals a key structural insight. The gradient and Hessian take the form

\[\rg[t] = \psi^2[t]\,\varepsilon[t], \qquad \rmH[t] = \psi^2[t].\]

In expectation,

\[\E[\rg[t]] = \lambda\,\E[\varepsilon[t]], \qquad \E[\rmH[t]] = \lambda.\]

Our primary objective in this analysis, is to drive the expected error \(\E[\varepsilon[t]]\) to converge the fastest to its optimum value of zero, starting from any initial non-zero value.

Now that the environment is set, we start with the classic algorithm, the plain stochastic gradient method.

Baseline: Plain Stochastic Gradient Method

At iteration $t$, this update rule

\[\varepsilon[t+1] = \varepsilon[t] - \gain{\alpha[t]}\,\rg[t]\]

receives a learning-rate estimate $\gain{\alpha[t]}$ from a learning-rate oracle and a gradient estimate $\rg[t]$ from a gradient-generating oracle. We will further assume that $\gain{\alpha[t]}=\gain{\alpha} > 0, \forall\, t$. Therefore

\[\varepsilon[t+1] = \varepsilon[t] - \gain{\alpha}\,\rg[t].\]

Next, we will see that ensuring mean convergence in this problem setup is equivalent to certifying the stability conditions of a linear iteration-invariant system.

Expected Error Dynamics

The expected error and expected error change for the plain SGM, forms a two-state dynamical system

\[\underbrace{ \begin{bmatrix} \E[\varepsilon[t]]\\ \E[\Delta[t+1]] \end{bmatrix}}_{\rvx[t]} = \underbrace{ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ k_p & a_p \end{bmatrix} }_{\rmA} \, \underbrace{ \begin{bmatrix} \E[\varepsilon[t-1]]\\ \E[\Delta[t]] \end{bmatrix}}_{\rvx[t-1]}\]

where \(a_p = -\gain{\alpha}\,\lambda\), and \(k_p = -\gain{\alpha}\,\lambda\). Reasons for separately naming the coefficients will soon become clear. The system can also be compactly written as

\[\rvx[t] = \rmA^t\,\rvx[0],\quad t \ge 0,\]

and is a rank-1 system because \(a_p = k_p\,\). The exponential stability and asymptotic decay to zero of the mean error variable and mean error change variable requires that the two eigenvalues of \(\rmA\) given by $z_1 = 0$, and \({z_2 =1-\gain{\alpha}\,\lambda}\) (which can be obtained via \(\det(z\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{A}) = z(z-(1-\gain{\alpha}\,\lambda)) = 0\)) to have a magnitude strictly less than 1 (Rugh, 1996). This stability condition is satisfied if and only if

\[0 < \gain{\alpha}\,\lambda < 2.\]

By taking any standard norm $\|\cdot\|$ (1-norm, 2-norm, $\infty$-norm) of $\rx[t]$, with the corresponding induced matrix norm, and then exploiting the rank-1 non-symmetric structure of \(\rmA\), a certificate on the exponential convergence of the expected error states, for \(t\ge 0\), can be obtained as

\[\begin{align} {\| \rvx[t+1] \| \le c \cdot |{z_2}|^{t}\, \|\rvx[0]\|}, \end{align}\]

where $c\ge 1$ is a positive constant determined by the left and right eigenvectors corresponding to the dominant (nonzero) eigenvalue \(z_2\), as well as on the chosen induced matrix norm of \(\rmA\).

Structural Insight

The fastest possible mode of convergence, $z_2 = 0$, is achieved by choosing

\[\gain{\alpha} = \tfrac{1}{\lambda}.\]

This yields deadbeat behavior (the expected error converges in a single iteration). However, deadbeat behavior of the expected error dynamics is generally undesirable in practice, and not ideal due to its high sensitivity to variability of the stochastic gradient.

Instead, directly minimizing the mean-squared error at the cost of a slower mean error contraction per iteration would result in

\[\gain{\alpha} = \mu\,\tfrac{1}{\lambda}, \qquad \mu = \tfrac{1}{3}.\]

The fundamental limitation here is structural. The mean error dynamics is inherently rank-1 for any choice of learning rate. To remove this limitation, we now regularize the gradient using a lowpass filter. By treating the gradient as a signal, we can transition to a much general algorithmic framework.

In signal processing, a lowpass filter attenuates high-frequency components of its input signal while preserving its low-frequency behavior. We will refer to this spectral shaping of the gradient as lowpass regularization.

The First-order Lowpass Filter

Now, instead of using gradient \(\rg[t]\), we replace it with a filtered version $\rv[t] = \filter{\sH_{\beta,\gamma}}\{\rg[t]\}$.

Transfer Function

The behavior of the filtered gradient is governed by the transfer-function

\[\filter{\sH_{\beta,\gamma}}(z) = \eta \frac{1-\filter{\gamma}\,z^{-1}}{1-\filter{\beta}\,z^{-1}}.\]

where \(z^{-1}\) denotes a one-step delay operator.

Lowpass Configuration

The filter has a single pole location $\beta$, zero location $\gamma$, and positive gain constant $\eta$ satisfying

\[\absv{\filter{\beta}} < 1, \quad \filter{\gamma} < \filter{\beta}, \quad \eta = \tfrac{1-\filter{\beta}}{1-\filter{\gamma}}\]

ensuring bounded-input, bounded-output (BIBO) stability, and a unit steady-state (or zero-frequency) gain.

Direct Realization

A direct difference equation realization of the first-order filter with input \(\rg[t]\) is

\[\begin{align*} \begin{aligned} \rv[t] &= \filter{\beta} \, \rv[t-1] + \eta\,\big(\rg[t] - \filter{\gamma} \, \rg[t-1] \big). \end{aligned} \end{align*}\]

The update rule in terms of the parameter error is now

\[\begin{align*} \begin{aligned} \varepsilon[t+1] &= \varepsilon[t] - \gain{\alpha}\,\rv[t]. \end{aligned} \end{align*}\]

However, note that this is an inefficient realization, since there are two states kept.

Direct Canonical Realization

For any choice of \((\filter{\beta}, \filter{\gamma})\), the direct minimal state canonical realization of the filter, where \(\rq[t]\) is the filter’s state is

\[\begin{align*} \begin{aligned} \rq[t] &= \filter{\beta}\,\rq[t-1] + \rg[t]\\ \rv[t] &= \filter{\eta}\bigl(\rq[t] - \filter{\gamma}\,q[t-1]\bigr)\\ \varepsilon[t+1] &= \varepsilon[t] - \gain{\alpha}\,\rv[t]. \end{aligned} \end{align*}\]

Note that $\eta$ can also be absorbed into the learning-rate.

Regularized Form

Compactly, for any appropriate initialization of \(\rq[-1]\), and denoting \(\delta=\filter{\beta}-\filter{\gamma}\), we have

\[\begin{align*} \begin{aligned} \varepsilon[t+1] &= \varepsilon[t] - \gain{\alpha}\,\filter{\eta}\,\Bigl(\rg[t] + \delta\,\filter{\beta}^{t}\rq[-1] + \delta\sum_{i=0}^{t-1}\filter{\beta}^{i}\,\rg[t-1-i]\Bigr),\quad t \ge 0. \end{aligned} \end{align*}\]

Non-Expansive Power Gain Property

Mathematically, this lowpass filter’s energy is non-expansive because its power gain

\[|\filter{\sH_{\beta,\gamma}}(e^{j\,\omega})|^2 \le 1,\]

for all frequencies \(\omega=[-\pi, \pi]\). This ensures that the filter’s output never contains more energy or variance than its input. Therefore

\[\E[\rv^2[t]] \le \E[\rg^2[t]].\]

The gradient filter spectrally regulates how gradient information is attenuated or amplified over iterations. With the filter smoothing the gradient input, we must now peek into how it reshapes the mean-error dynamics.

Full-Rank Regularization of the Expected Error Dynamics

With the lowpass filter, the system matrix \(\rmA\) still retains its two-state form, \(\rmA = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ k_p & a_p \end{bmatrix}.\)

However, the constants \(k_p\neq a_p\), indicating that the introduction of gradient filtering transforms \(\rmA\) into a full-rank system, where

\[a_p = \filter{\beta}-\gain{\alpha}\eta\lambda, \quad k_p = -\gain{\alpha}\eta\lambda(1-\filter{\gamma}).\]

The characteristic polynomial is \(z^2 - (1+a_p)z + (a_p - k_p) = 0.\) The stability of this system is determined by the eigenvalues or roots \((z_1, z_2)\) of \(\rmA\). Since this is a quadratic equation, the discriminant: \(D = (1 + a_p)^2 - 4(a_p - k_p)\) can either be:

  • $D = 0$: (two real, repeated roots),
  • $D > 0$: (two real, distinct roots), or
  • $D < 0$: (two complex, conjugate roots).

Stability Certificate

For stability, the eigenvalues must remain within the unit circle: \(|z_1| \le |z_2| < 1.\)

Fastest Convergence

The fastest mode of convergence (\(z_1 = z_2 = 0\)) of the expected error dynamics occurs if and only if both \(1+a_p =0\) and \(a_p-k_p=0.\) This leads to the following configuration.

Choose the of the zero location of the filter as

\[\filter{\gamma} = \frac{\filter{\beta}}{1+\filter{\beta}}\]

and the learning rate such that:

\[\gain{\alpha} \eta \lambda = 1 + \filter{\beta}\]

for any choice of the filter pole $\absv{\filter{\beta}} < 1$.

To see the realization of this mean-error optimal filter zero configuration in action

Result: Nesterov Momentum (NAG)

Plugging \(\filter{\gamma}=\frac{\filter{\beta}}{1+\filter{\beta}}\) into the canonical filter realization yields

\[\begin{aligned} &\rq[t] = \filter{\beta} \, \rq[t-1] + (1-\filter{\beta})\,\rg[t]\\ &\rv[t] = \filter{\beta}\,\rq[t] + (1-\filter{\beta})\,\rg[t]\\ &\varepsilon[t+1] = \varepsilon[t] - \gain{\alpha} \, \rv[t]. \end{aligned}\]

Equivalently, the \(1-\filter{\beta}\) factor can be absorbed into the learning-rate yielding \(\bar{\gain{\alpha}}=\gain{\alpha}(1-\filter{\beta})\) for any choice of $\gain{\alpha}$. The canonical realization then becomes

\[\begin{aligned} &\rq[t] = \filter{\beta} \, \rq[t-1] + \rg[t]\\ &\rv[t] = \filter{\beta}\,\rq[t] + \rg[t]\\ &\varepsilon[t+1] = \varepsilon[t] - \bar{\gain{\alpha}} \, \rv[t], \end{aligned}\]

These realizations of the first-order lowpass filter \(\sH_{\filter{\beta},\filter{\gamma}}\) acting on \(\rg[t]\) have been called Nesterov Accelerated Gradient (NAG). Therefore, the popular Nesterov momentum realization of the parameter update is exactly recovered when \(\filter{\gamma}=\frac{\filter{\beta}}{1+\filter{\beta}}\).

Using the deadbeat learning-rate, \(\bar{\gain{\alpha}}\) simplifies to \(\bar{\gain{\alpha}} = \frac{1}{\lambda}\).

Stability Region

We can analyze the characteristic polynomial using Jury stability conditions, to get that for \(0 \le \filter{\beta} < 1\):

\[0 < \gain{\alpha} \eta \lambda < \frac{2(1+\filter{\beta})^2}{1+2\filter{\beta}}\]

We now compare a trivial configuration of the zero location of the filter, $\filter{\gamma} = 0$.

Compare: Heavy-Ball Momentum (HB)

Now consider the filter design corresponding to $\filter{\gamma}=0$ and $0\le\filter{\beta}<1$. This leads to $a_p-k_p=\filter{\beta}$, and the characteristic polynomial $z^2 -(1+a_p)\,z + (a_p-k_p) = 0 $ simplifies to $z^2 - (1+a_p)\,z + \filter{\beta} = 0 $ with discriminant $D = (1+a_p)^2 - 4\,\filter{\beta}$. Setting $1+a_p =1+\filter{\beta}-\gain{\alpha}\eta\lambda=0$ gives $\gain{\alpha}\eta\lambda={1+\filter{\beta}}$, the characteristic polynomial becomes $z^2 + \filter{\beta} = 0 $ and the discriminant is minimized as $D =-4\filter{\beta}$.

With this choice, the two system eigenvalues of \(\rmA\) satisfy a common magnitude \(\lvert z_1 \rvert = \lvert z_2 \rvert = \sqrt{\absv{\filter{\beta}}}< 1,\) which ensures exponential stability with decay rate $\sqrt{\absv{\filter{\beta}}}<1$.

For the lowpass filter case $0<\filter{\beta}<1$, the eigenvalues form a complex‑conjugate pair $z_{1,2}=\pm\,j\sqrt{\filter{\beta}}$, corresponding to $D=-4\filter{\beta}<0$.

For the no filtering case $\filter{\beta}=0$, the eigenvalues degenerate to repeated values $\lvert z_1 \rvert = \lvert z_2 \rvert= 0$ yielding the fastest possible expected error decay and so $D=-4\filter{\beta}=0$.

Generally, the characteristic polynomial $z^2 - (1+a_p)\,z + \beta=0$ satisfies $z_1+z_2 = 1+a_p$ and $z_1\,z_2=\filter{\beta}$. Solving the discriminant $D\le 0$ for $\gain{\alpha}$ leads to the learning-rate interval

\[\tfrac{(1-\sqrt{\absv{\filter{\beta}}})^2}{\eta}\, \tfrac{1}{\lambda} \le \gain{\alpha} \le \tfrac{(1+\sqrt{\absv{\filter{\beta}}})^2}{\eta}\, \tfrac{1}{\lambda}.\]

The midpoint of this interval $\gain{\alpha} = \frac{1+\filter{\beta}}{\eta}\, \frac{1}{\lambda}= \frac{1+\filter{\beta}}{1-\filter{\beta}}\,\frac{1}{\lambda}$ corresponds to $1+a_p=0$.

Consequently, as $\filter{\beta} \to 0$, and $\gain{\alpha} \to \frac{1}{\lambda}$ then both the plain SGM update form and the fastest possible mean convergence mode is recovered in the limit.

In summary, the filter design choice $\filter{\gamma}=0$ and $0\le\filter{\beta}<1$ induces a mean error dynamics whose exponential stability or decay rate depends solely on the pole location ${\filter{\beta}}$.

To implement this filter design of the parameter update, the canonical realization yields

\[\begin{aligned} &\rv[t] = \filter{\beta}\,\rv[t-1] + (1-\filter{\beta})\,\rg[t]\\ &\varepsilon[t+1] = \varepsilon[t] - \gain{\alpha} \, \rv[t], \end{aligned}\]

which recovers the Heavy-Ball (HB) momentum form of the parameter update. Equivalently

\[\begin{aligned} &\rv[t] = \filter{\beta}\,\rv[t-1] + \rg[t]\\ &\varepsilon[t+1] = \varepsilon[t] - \bar{\gain{\alpha}} \, \rv[t], \end{aligned}\]

where $\bar{\gain{\alpha}}=\gain{\alpha}(1-\filter{\beta})$.

Recall that here, the first moment of the gradient is $\E[\rg[t]] = \lambda\,\E[\varepsilon[t]]$.

The output of momentum, being the result of a lowpass filter applied to the gradient sequence, is in general neither equal nor pointwise close to the gradient nor to its expectation at any iteration, except in the degenerate case of exact pole-zero cancellation: $\gamma=\beta$, under which $\filter{\sH_{\beta,\gamma}}(z)$ reduces to an effective all-pass filter response.

The statistical interpretation of the filter as a first-moment estimator arises only by replacing the exponentially weighted convolution in the iteration-domain with an ensemble expectation. This action explicitly requires an ergodicity assumption so that a weighted time-average along a single realization of the stochastic process can be identified with an ensemble mean.

Therefore, in general and especially in non-stationary stochastic gradient regimes, ergodic assumptions are not valid. Consequently, the first moment estimation interpretation breaks down mathematically, while the lowpass filtering characterization of momentum always remains exact and assumption-free.

Stability Region

Here, for \(\gamma=0,\, 0 \le \filter{\beta} < 1\):

\[0 < \gain{\alpha} {\eta} \lambda < 2(1+\filter{\beta})\]

Takeaways

The two pillars of momentum, Polyak’s Heavy-Ball (HB) and Nesterov Accelerated Gradient (NAG), are often viewed as distinct algorithms. Through our filter lens, however, they are simply different configurations of the same system. Polyak’s Heavy-Ball (HB) and Nesterov Accelerated Gradient (NAG) are specific choices of the zero location $\filter{\gamma}$ in a lowpass filter, while Plain SGM places both the pole and zero of this filter at the origin ($\filter{\gamma}=\filter{\beta}=0$).

Acceleration Isn’t Always the Point

Due to roots in classical mechanics, we often think that the momentum here exists to accelerate optimization. As observed in this setup, using momentum does not lead to a faster mean convergence than the plain stochastic gradient method. The fastest possible mean convergence can be achieved without momentum.

So why use momentum? Trivally, because stochastic gradients imply a rough, noisy error surface. Momentum is a lowpass filter, attenuating high-frequency noise.

Contrary to popular belief, the principal benefit of momentum is not in acceleration alone, but in dynamical regularization of the parameter update steps. Our position here is that momentum’s primary benefit is dynamic regularization.

In general, we see that by regularizing the gradient via the lowpass filter, we get the following properties:

  1. Noise Attenuation (Spectral Smoothing) preserving the low-frequency structure in the data process while reducing high-frequency noise variance in the gradient
  2. Energy Non-Expansiveness ensuring the update rule never amplifies noise in the gradient.
  3. Expanded Stability Region increases the allowable range of \(\alpha\) before divergence.

Open Question

If momentum is just a linear first-order filter, what unexplored data efficient, nonlinear filter designs could unlock unseen optimization gains for AI?

Reality Check

These insights are derived under simplifying assumptions. Real deep learning systems are:

  • High-dimensional
  • Non-stationary
  • Heavy-tailed

Therefore, note that in real settings, the:

  • Structural roles (filtering structure + learning-rate form) generalize
  • Optimal parameter values do not.

Conclusion

By understanding momentum as a signal processing filter, we replace the concept of momentum with interpretable pole-zero placements that dynamically regularize gradient information entering our stochastic gradient optimization algorithms.
