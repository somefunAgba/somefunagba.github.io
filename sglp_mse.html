Momentum as Lowpass Regularization

Fastest mean-square convergence can be achieved without Momentum.

Sep 11 2025

Please cite this page if you use information from these notes for your work, research, or anything that requires academic or formal citation. Oluwasegun Somefun. “Momentum as Lowpass Regularization.” 2025.

  1. Momentum as Lowpass Regularization

Momentum (Polyak, 1969) is a lowpass regularized stochastic gradient algorithm. One that applies a first-order lowpass filter to its stochastic gradient, before multiplying by a possibly optimal iteration-dependent learning rate. For analytic purpose, we study a minimal stochastic system, for which the algorithm’s hyperparameters are assumed time-invariant. More specifically, we show how momentum cannot improve over the fastest mean-square error convergence of the Plain stochastic gradient method in this setup.

Understanding Momentum as Lowpass Regularization

Traditionally, the way we talk about training neural networks is often rooted in classical mechanics. We describe momentum as a ball rolling down a hilly landscape, gaining inertia to carry it over local bumps. Alternatively, another popular interpretation is that momentum estimates the first moment of a gradient. While these physical analogies are often intuitive, they obscure the underlying discrete, iteration-level mechanics of the learning algorithm.

Our central claim is that a literal mathematical interpretation already exists via classical signal processing tools related to first-order lowpass filters. Momentum is mathematically equivalent to passing stochastic gradients through a first-order linear lowpass filter.

The filter, denoted \(\filter{\sH_{\beta,\gamma}}\) is configured via two variables, its pole \(\filter{\beta}\) and zero \(\filter{\gamma}\). By shifting the pole and zero locations of the filter, we can create different flavors of momentum.

We will soon see that this filter design interpretation provides a principled dynamical regularization perspective into the behavior of classic momentum variants, and can improve the way we design stochastic gradient learning algorithms for training AI models.

Setup: A Minimalist Stochastic Error Model

To understand what fundamentally drives learning dynamics, it helps to strip the system down to its bare essentials. To make our position concrete and analytically transparent, we deliberately adopt a minimalist setting. By stripping away the tensor algebra of high-dimensional neural networks, we expose the error dynamics and underlying structure of stochastic gradient learning in its simplest form.

Consider a single parameter model defined by the twice-differentiable weighted quadratic function

\[f(\rw[t]) = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\psi^2[t]\,\varepsilon^2[t],\]

where

  • \(\rw[t] \in \sR^{1}\) (Parameter) denotes the model parameter at a current iteration \(t\).
  • \(\varepsilon[t]\) (Error) defined as \(\varepsilon[t]=\rw[t] - \rw^\star\) represents distance of the current parameter value from its optimum value, \(\rw^\star\). The current error change due to a parameter update is defined as \(\Delta[t+1]=\varepsilon[t+1]- \varepsilon[t]\).
  • \(\psi[t] \in \sR^{1}\) (Data Process) is a realization of a zero-mean, wide-sense stationary stochastic data process for which its kurtosis, \(\kappa\ge1\) exists. We also assume that \(\psi[t]\) is statistically independent of both \(\varepsilon[t]\) and \(\psi[l]\), $l \neq t$.
  • \(\lambda\) (Scale) denotes the second moment of the stochastic process, defined by \(\mathbb{E}[\psi^2[t]]=\lambda\), and \(\mathbb{E}[\psi^4[t]]=\kappa\,\lambda^2\), \(\lambda>0\).

Differentiating \(f(\rw[t])\) with respect to \(\rw[t]\) reveals a key structural insight. The gradient and Hessian take the form

\[\rg[t] = \psi^2[t]\,\varepsilon[t], \qquad \rmH[t] = \psi^2[t].\]

In expectation,

\[\E[\rg[t]] = \lambda\,\E[\varepsilon[t]], \qquad \E[\rmH[t]] = \lambda.\]

Our primary objective in this analysis, is to drive the expected objective-function to converge the fastest to its optimum value of zero, starting from any initial non-zero value.
